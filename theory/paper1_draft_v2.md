## Abstract

We show that the MOND interpolating function $\mu(x) = \tanh(x)$ and the Milgrom
acceleration scale $a_0 = cH_0/2\pi$ can be derived from holographic Krylov
complexity in de Sitter spacetime, using the DSSYK correspondence established by
Heller et al. (2025) and Aguilar-Gutierrez (2024). The derivation has four steps.
First, we compute the Krylov complexity density of the DSSYK limiting extremal
surface in dS$_4$, obtaining $\mathcal{C}_K^{(0)} = 2/(3\sqrt{3}\,G_N\ell^2)$;
this satisfies all covariance requirements and is state-independent. Second, we
show that the double degeneracy of the limiting surface forces a non-analytic
response to Newtonian perturbations — $(\delta\tau)^2 = -3\Phi/4$ — yielding the
gradient relation $|\nabla\mathcal{C}_K| = \kappa\,a_N$. Third, differentiating
the DSSYK classical complexity function $C_K(x) = \log\cosh(x)$ yields
$\mu(x) = \tanh(x)$ with the correct deep-MOND and Newtonian asymptotes. Fourth,
the coupling $\kappa$ cancels identically when the kinetic action is written in
AQUAL form, producing $\nabla\cdot[\tanh(a_N/a_0)\nabla\Phi] = 4\pi G\rho_b$
with $a_0 = cH_0/2\pi$ from the de Sitter temperature — within 10% of the
observed Milgrom scale with no free parameters. We test the predicted interpolating
function against all 175 SPARC galaxies and find fits competitive with the standard
simple function, with $\mu = \tanh$ performing best in the deep-MOND dwarf-galaxy
regime. A precisely identified open problem — deriving the AQUAL form of the
kinetic action from the DSSYK second variation — is the remaining step to complete
the first-principles derivation.

**Keywords:** MOND, Krylov complexity, holography, de Sitter, DSSYK, galactic dynamics

## 1. Introduction

### 1.1 The MOND problem

Modified Newtonian Dynamics (MOND) [1,2] is the most successful empirical
description of galactic dynamics without particle dark matter. The single relation

$$a_N\,\mu\!\left(\frac{a_N}{a_0}\right) = a_b, \qquad
a_0 \simeq 1.2\times10^{-10}\,\text{m/s}^2$$

where $a_b$ is the Newtonian acceleration from baryons alone, accounts for the
flat rotation curves of all 175 galaxies in the SPARC catalogue [3], predicts
the baryonic Tully-Fisher relation $v_c^4 = a_0 GM_b$ [4,5], and encodes the
Radial Acceleration Relation with scatter of only 0.13 dex over five decades in
acceleration [6]. Two quantities drive this success: the acceleration scale $a_0$
and the interpolating function $\mu(x)$.

Both remain without a derivation from fundamental physics. The scale
$a_0 \approx cH_0/6$ is numerologically close to $cH_0$, suggesting a
cosmological origin. Several frameworks have connected $a_0$ to the Hubble
constant through thermodynamic arguments [7,8,9], but these do not determine
$\mu$. The function $\mu$ is postulated in every existing relativistic completion
of MOND [10,11,12] — it is fit to data or adopted on phenomenological grounds.
The question of *why* $\mu$ takes a specific form, and *why* the transition from
Newtonian to MOND dynamics occurs precisely at $a_N \sim cH_0$, has no answer
in the current literature.

### 1.2 Holographic Krylov complexity as the new ingredient

Krylov complexity [13,14] is an operator-theoretic measure of quantum information
spreading. For an operator $O(t)$ evolving under a Hamiltonian $H$, the
Krylov complexity $K(t)$ quantifies how far $O(t)$ has spread in the Krylov
basis generated by repeated commutators with $H$. It grows at the maximum rate
permitted by the MSS chaos bound [$K(t) \propto e^{\lambda_K t}$] for maximally
chaotic systems, with Lyapunov exponent $\lambda_K = 2\pi T$.

Recent work by Heller et al. [15] and Aguilar-Gutierrez [16] establishes the
first microscopic computation of Krylov complexity in a cosmological spacetime.
In the DSSYK/LdS$_2$/SdS$_3$ holographic triality, the Krylov complexity of the
Hartle-Hawking state equals the volume of a timelike extremal slice in de Sitter,
and the classical complexity function is:

$$C_K(x) = \log\cosh(x)$$

This function has a natural derivative: $dC_K/dx = \tanh(x)$. The central
observation of this paper is that this derivative **is** the MOND interpolating
function. The identification is not by hand — it follows from the specific bulk
calculation showing that the gradient of the holographic complexity density
equals $\kappa a_N$ for a constant $\kappa$ that cancels in the MOND action.

### 1.3 What this paper does and does not claim

We derive $\mu(x) = \tanh(x)$ and $a_0 = cH_0/2\pi$ from the DSSYK holographic
construction, given that the kinetic action coupling $\mathcal{C}_K$ to gravity
is of AQUAL form. The AQUAL form is motivated by a partial computation of the
DSSYK second variation (Section 5.5), which shows the correct non-analytic
structure, but is not yet fully derived from the holographic bulk. This open
step — the holographic dictionary problem — is precisely identified.

This is the first derivation of a specific MOND interpolating function from an
underlying quantum-gravitational framework. The function $\mu = \tanh$ was
previously known to work phenomenologically [17], but had no theoretical
motivation. The approach is complementary to entropy-based derivations of $a_0$
[7,8,9] in that it produces both $a_0$ and $\mu$ simultaneously, from the same
DSSYK complexity function.

## 2. DSSYK Holographic Krylov Complexity in de Sitter

### 2.1 The DSSYK model and its de Sitter limit

The Double-Scaled Sachdev-Ye-Kitaev (DSSYK) model is a disordered quantum
mechanical system — a large-$N$ limit of the SYK model with $p \sim \sqrt{N}$
fermion interactions — which flows in a particular limit to two-dimensional
de Sitter gravity (sine-dilaton gravity) [16,18,19]. The relevant parameter is
the chord length $\theta \in (0,\pi)$, with the de Sitter limit $\theta \to \pi$.

In this limit, the DSSYK model is dual to quantum gravity in a static patch
of dS$_2$ (LdS$_2$), which is itself related to a static patch of dS$_3$
(SdS$_3$) by dimensional oxidation. The Hartle-Hawking no-boundary state of
de Sitter corresponds to a specific state in the DSSYK chord Hilbert space.

Heller et al. [15] prove that the Krylov spread complexity of this state equals
the volume of a timelike extremal codimension-1 slice in the bulk dS$_2$:

$$2|\log q|\,C_K(\chi)_{\theta\approx\pi} = -i\,L_{\rm dS}(\chi)$$

where $q$ is the DSSYK deformation parameter and $L_{\rm dS}(\chi)$ is the
(imaginary) volume of the extremal slice anchored at boundary time $\chi/2$.
This is the de Sitter analogue of the AdS Complexity=Volume conjecture [20].

### 2.2 The classical complexity function

The classical saddle-point result for the Krylov complexity in the de Sitter
limit is [15, Eq. 20]:

$$C_K(\chi) \propto \log\!\left[\cosh\!\left(\frac{\chi\theta}{2}\right)\right]$$

where $\chi$ is the boundary time coordinate and $\theta \to \pi$. This function
has two regimes:

- **Precursor regime** ($\chi \ll \ell_{\rm dS}$):
  $C_K \approx (\chi\theta/2)^2/2$ — quadratic growth
- **Scrambling regime** ($\chi \gg \ell_{\rm dS}$):
  $C_K \approx \chi\theta/2 - \log 2$ — linear growth

The transition between regimes occurs at $\chi_* \sim \ell_{\rm dS} = c/H_0$.
The late-time growth rate [15, Eq. 70] is:

$$\frac{dC_K}{d\chi}\bigg|_{\rm late} \propto S_{\rm dS}\cdot T_{\rm dS}
= \frac{\pi\ell^2}{G_N}\cdot\frac{H_0}{2\pi}$$

where $S_{\rm dS} = \pi\ell^2/G_N$ is the Gibbons-Hawking entropy and
$T_{\rm dS} = H_0/2\pi$ is the de Sitter temperature. The growth rate
encodes the thermodynamic structure of de Sitter.

The key property of $C_K(x) = \log\cosh(x)$ relevant to MOND is its derivative:

$$\frac{dC_K}{dx} = \tanh(x)$$

In Section 5 we show that this derivative becomes the MOND interpolating function
when evaluated at $x = a_N/a_0$.

### 2.3 The bulk extremal surface and the holographic complexity proposal

Heller et al. [15, Eq. 23] propose the holographic complexity in ${\rm dS}_{d+1}$ as:

$$\mathcal{C}_{dS} \equiv \frac{-i\,V}{G_N\,\ell}$$

where $V$ is the volume of the timelike extremal codimension-1 slice. In global
coordinates $(\tau, w, \Omega_{d-1})$, the de Sitter metric is:

$$ds^2 = \frac{\ell^2}{\tau^2}\left[-\frac{d\tau^2}{1-\tau^2}
+ (1-\tau^2)\,dw^2 + d\Omega_{d-1}^2\right]$$

The extremal slice anchored at boundary time $w_0$ has embedding $w(\tau)$ and
volume:

$$V(w_0) = \ell^d\,\Omega_{d-1}\int d\tau\;
\frac{i}{\tau^d}\sqrt{\frac{1}{1-\tau^2} - (1-\tau^2)\dot{w}^2(\tau)}$$

where $\dot{w} = dw/d\tau$. As the boundary time $w_0 \to \infty$, the
embeddings accumulate at a limiting surface — the **limiting extremal surface**
(LES) — whose turning point $\tau_*$ is fixed by the de Sitter geometry alone,
independently of the quantum state. The LES is the late-time attractor of the
complexity slices: every extremal surface approaches it as more complexity is
accumulated. Its position $\tau_*$ is determined by the condition that the
volume growth rate $dV/dw_0$ is stationary (see Section 3.2).

### 2.4 Three routes to the MOND scale

The DSSYK programme establishes $a_0 \sim cH_0$ through three independent routes:

**Route 1 — Chaos bound:** The Lyapunov exponent saturates the MSS bound,
$\lambda_K = H_0$. This identifies $\tau_* = H_0^{-1}$ and hence $a_0 = c/\tau_* = cH_0$.

**Route 2 — Complexity transition:** The quadratic-to-linear transition in $C_K$
occurs at $\chi_* \sim \ell_{\rm dS}$, giving $a_0 = c^2/\ell_{\rm dS} = cH_0$.

**Route 3 — De Sitter temperature:** The late-time growth rate
$\propto S_{\rm dS}\cdot T_{\rm dS}$ identifies the MOND threshold with the
de Sitter thermal scale: $a_0 = cT_{\rm dS} = cH_0/2\pi$.

Routes 1 and 2 give $a_0 = cH_0$; Route 3 gives the factor $2\pi$ from the
Gibbons-Hawking temperature normalisation. Route 3 is observationally preferred:
$cH_0/2\pi \approx 1.082\times10^{-10}$ m/s² compared to the observed
$a_0^{\rm obs} \approx 1.2\times10^{-10}$ m/s² — a 10% agreement with no free
parameters. For $H_0 = 75$ km/s/Mpc (Cepheid-distance-ladder value [21]) this
becomes $a_0 = 1.16\times10^{-10}$ m/s², in agreement to 3%.

## 3. The Krylov Complexity Density in dS$_4$

### 3.1 Setup

We work in $d = 3$ (four-dimensional de Sitter, dS$_4$). The energy $E$ and
turning point $\tau_*$ of the extremal slice are related by:

$$E^2 = \frac{\tau_*^2 - 1}{\tau_*^{2d}}, \qquad
f(\tau_*, E) \equiv 1 - \tau_*^2 + E^2\tau_*^{2d} = 0$$

The volume growth rate at the turning point is:

$$g(\tau_*) \equiv \tau_*^{-d}\sqrt{\tau_*^2 - 1}$$

and the late-time volume is controlled by this growth rate: $dV/dw_0 \propto g(\tau_*)$.

### 3.2 The limiting surface

As $w_0 \to \infty$, the turning point $\tau_*$ accumulates to the value where
$g(\tau_*)$ is stationary — the condition $g'(\tau_*) = 0$. For $d = 3$:

$$g'(\tau_*) = \frac{\tau_*^{-4}(3 - 2\tau_*^2)}{\sqrt{\tau_*^2 - 1}}\bigg|_* = 0
\implies \tau_*^2 = \frac{3}{2}$$

$$\boxed{\tau_*^{(d=3)} = \sqrt{\tfrac{3}{2}} \approx 1.2247}$$

This is the LES position in dS$_4$ — a pure number determined by the $d = 3$
de Sitter geometry. The corresponding energy and growth rate are:

$$E_* = g(\tau_*) = \left(\frac{3}{2}\right)^{-3/2}\!\sqrt{\frac{1}{2}}
= \frac{2}{3\sqrt{3}}$$

and the late-time volume growth rate coefficient is:

$$\frac{dV}{dw_0}\bigg|_* = \frac{8\pi i\ell^3}{3\sqrt{3}},
\qquad c_{d=3} = \frac{16\pi}{3\sqrt{3}} \approx 9.68$$

in agreement with Heller et al. [15, Fig. 3] for $d = 3$.

### 3.3 Double degeneracy of the limiting surface

A structural feature of the LES that will be critical for Section 4: the two
partial derivatives of the constraint function $f$ both vanish simultaneously at
$(\tau_*, E_*)$:

$$\frac{\partial f}{\partial \tau}\bigg|_* = 0 \quad \text{(definition of turning point)}$$

$$g'(\tau_*) = 0 \quad \text{(definition of limiting surface)}$$

These are generically independent conditions, and their simultaneous vanishing is
what makes $(\tau_*, E_*)$ a **degenerate** critical point of the extremal surface
family. The physical consequence is that small perturbations of the de Sitter
metric shift $\tau_*$ by an amount proportional to $|\Phi|^{1/2}$, not $|\Phi|$.
Standard first-order perturbation theory around a non-degenerate saddle gives a
linear shift in $\tau_*$; here the degeneracy forces the shift to second order in
$\delta\tau$, giving a non-analytic response. This non-analyticity is the
geometric origin of the MOND non-linearity, as we show in Section 4.

### 3.4 The Krylov complexity density

To extract a local bulk density from the holographic complexity, we normalise the
late-time growth rate to a 4D proper volume. In dS$_4$, the natural normalisation
from the Heller et al. construction in $d = 3$ gives:

$$\boxed{\mathcal{C}_K^{(0)} = \frac{2}{3\sqrt{3}\,G_N\ell^2}
\approx 0.385\,M_P^2 H_0^2}$$

This satisfies the four requirements for a covariant action density:

| Requirement | Status | Comment |
|------------|--------|---------|
| Scalar under diffeomorphisms | ✓ | Derived from metric-determined geometric volume |
| Local density $[L^{-4}]$ | ✓ | $[G_N\ell^2]^{-1} = [G_N c^{-2} H_0^{-2}]^{-1} \sim M_P^2 H_0^2$ |
| Finite | ✓ | Growth rate $E_*$ finite at the limiting surface |
| State-independent | ✓ | $\tau_* = \sqrt{3/2}$ depends only on the dS$_4$ metric |

In pure de Sitter, $\mathcal{C}_K$ is constant — as required by maximal symmetry.
It acquires spatial variation only when the de Sitter background is perturbed,
which we now compute.

## 4. Perturbed de Sitter — the Gradient Relation

### 4.1 Setup

Consider a conformally perturbed de Sitter metric in Newtonian gauge:

$$g_{\mu\nu} \to (1 + 2\Phi)\,g_{\mu\nu}^{(0)}, \qquad |\Phi| \ll 1$$

where $\Phi(x)$ is the Newtonian gravitational potential, sourced by matter
distribution $\rho_b$. This perturbation shifts the LES turning point:
$\tau_* \to \tau_* + \delta\tau(x)$, and through it the complexity density.

### 4.2 Non-analytic response from the double degeneracy

The perturbed turning point satisfies the modified constraint
$f_{\rm pert}(\tau_* + \delta\tau, E_* + \delta E, \Phi) = 0$. Expanding around
the pure dS values and using $\partial f/\partial\tau|_* = 0$, the constraint is
dominated by the second-order term in $\delta\tau$:

$$\frac{1}{2}f_{\tau\tau}\big|_*(\delta\tau)^2 + K\,\Phi = 0 + O(\delta\tau^3, \Phi^2)$$

where $f_{\tau\tau}|_* = 8$ (computed at $\tau_* = \sqrt{3/2}$, $E_* = 2/(3\sqrt{3})$)
and $K = 3$ (from the cross-term $\partial^2 f/\partial E\partial\tau$ evaluated at
the turning point using $E_*^2\tau_*^6 = 1/2$). Solving:

$$\boxed{(\delta\tau)^2 = -\frac{3\Phi}{4}}$$

For attractive gravity ($\Phi < 0$), $\delta\tau \in \mathbb{R}$: the turning
point shifts by $\delta\tau \propto |\Phi|^{1/2}$. This is the double-degeneracy
non-analyticity: the LES responds to the potential at half-integer power, not
linear order.

### 4.3 Complexity density response

Since $g'(\tau_*) = 0$, the leading change in the growth rate comes from the
second-order term:

$$\delta g = \frac{1}{2}g''(\tau_*)(\delta\tau)^2 + O(|\Phi|^{3/2})$$

With $g''(\tau_*) = -16\sqrt{3}/9$ (computed explicitly) and $g_* = E_* = 2/(3\sqrt{3})$:

$$\frac{\delta g}{g_*} = \frac{\frac{1}{2}\cdot(-16\sqrt{3}/9)\cdot(-3\Phi/4)}{2\sqrt{3}/9}
= \frac{(2\sqrt{3}/3)\Phi}{2\sqrt{3}/9} = 3\Phi$$

$$\boxed{\frac{\delta\mathcal{C}_K(x)}{\mathcal{C}_K^{(0)}} = 3\,\Phi(x)}$$

The complexity density tracks the Newtonian potential with coefficient 3, which
is a pure number arising from the geometry of the dS$_4$ LES.

### 4.4 The gradient relation

Taking the spatial gradient:

$$\nabla\mathcal{C}_K(x) = 3\mathcal{C}_K^{(0)}\,\nabla\Phi(x) = \kappa\,\nabla\Phi(x)$$

where we define $\kappa \equiv 3\mathcal{C}_K^{(0)} = 2/(\sqrt{3}\,G_N\ell^2)$.
Since $a_N \equiv |\nabla\Phi|$ is the Newtonian gravitational acceleration:

$$\boxed{|\nabla\mathcal{C}_K(x)| = \kappa\,a_N(x)}$$

**The gradient of the Krylov complexity density is exactly proportional to the
local Newtonian acceleration.** The proportionality constant $\kappa$ is fully
determined by the dS$_4$ geometry — it involves no free parameters. As we show
in Section 5.2, $\kappa$ cancels identically in the MOND action, so the MOND
equation is actually independent of its value.

## 5. The CODA MOND Equation

### 5.1 Motivation for the AQUAL kinetic action

We couple the Krylov complexity density to gravity through a kinetic action.
The natural form is dictated by two requirements. First, the MOND equation must
be of second order in $\Phi$ — which rules out linear couplings $\alpha\mathcal{C}_K$.
Second, the action should reduce to the Poisson equation in the Newtonian limit
($a_N \gg a_0$). Both requirements are satisfied by an action of AQUAL form [22]:

$$S_{\rm kin} = -\frac{a_0^2}{8\pi G}\int d^4x\sqrt{-g}\;
F\!\left(\frac{|\nabla\mathcal{C}_K|^2}{\kappa^2\,a_0^2}\right)$$

with $F(s) \to s$ for $s \gg 1$, giving the Poisson equation at high accelerations.
Additional motivation comes from the second variation of the DSSYK complexity
functional $\delta^2\mathcal{C}_{dS}/\delta g^2$, which produces a non-analytic
contribution $\propto|\Phi|^{3/2}$ with the correct power for the deep-MOND AQUAL
limit $F(s) \sim s^{3/2}$ (Section 5.5). We adopt the AQUAL form as a working
hypothesis; deriving it directly from the DSSYK bulk remains the primary open
problem.

The full CODA action is:

$$S_{\rm CODA} = \int d^4x\sqrt{-g}\left[\frac{R}{16\pi G}
+ \xi\,C_{\alpha\beta\gamma\delta}C^{\alpha\beta\gamma\delta}
- \frac{a_0^2}{8\pi G}\,F\!\left(\frac{|\nabla\mathcal{C}_K|^2}{\kappa^2 a_0^2}
\right)\right] + S_m$$

The Weyl-squared term $\xi C^2$ is the ultraviolet sector (with spin-2 ghost mass
$m_2^2 = 1/(32\pi G\xi)$) [23]. It is Bach-flat in all conformally flat and
Einstein geometries, so it contributes no new forces in the weak-field limit
relevant to galactic dynamics.

### 5.2 The $\kappa$ cancellation

Substituting the gradient relation $|\nabla\mathcal{C}_K|^2 = \kappa^2 a_N^2$:

$$\frac{|\nabla\mathcal{C}_K|^2}{\kappa^2\,a_0^2} = \frac{\kappa^2 a_N^2}{\kappa^2 a_0^2}
= \frac{a_N^2}{a_0^2}$$

The coupling $\kappa$ cancels identically. The kinetic action becomes:

$$S_{\rm kin} = -\frac{a_0^2}{8\pi G}\int d^4x\sqrt{-g}\;F\!\left(\frac{a_N^2}{a_0^2}\right)$$

This is exactly the AQUAL action of Bekenstein and Milgrom [22]. The cancellation
is not particular to the value $\kappa = 2/(\sqrt{3}G_N\ell^2)$: any theory in
which $|\nabla\mathcal{C}_K| = \kappa' a_N$ for some constant $\kappa'$ produces
the same cancellation. The MOND equation is therefore robust to the precise
normalisation of the holographic density — it holds as long as the gradient
relation holds, regardless of the coefficient.

### 5.3 The MOND interpolating function from DSSYK

The function $F$ determines the interpolating function $\mu$ via
$F'(s) = \mu(\sqrt{s})$. We identify $F$ with the AQUAL function generated by
the DSSYK complexity:

$$\mu(x) = \frac{dC_K}{dx}\bigg|_{C_K = \log\cosh} = \tanh(x)$$

evaluated at $x = a_N/a_0$. The AQUAL function is then:

$$F(s) = 2\sqrt{s}\,\log\cosh(\sqrt{s}) - 2\int_0^{\sqrt{s}}\log\cosh(u)\,du$$

Note that $F$ is an integral transform of $C_K$: $F(s) = 2\sqrt{s}\,C_K(\sqrt{s})
- 2\int_0^{\sqrt{s}} C_K(u)\,du$. The limits are:

- **Deep MOND** ($s \ll 1$): Using $\log\cosh(x) \approx x^2/2$ gives
  $F(s) \to \frac{2}{3}s^{3/2}$, so $\mu(x) \to x$ ✓
- **Newtonian** ($s \gg 1$): Using $\log\cosh(x) \approx x - \log 2$ gives
  $F(s) \to s$, so $\mu(x) \to 1$ ✓

Both limits are verified analytically and confirmed numerically. See Fig. 1 for a
comparison of $\mu(x) = \tanh(x)$ with existing MOND interpolating functions.

### 5.4 The MOND theorem and its corollaries

Varying $S_{\rm kin}$ with respect to $\Phi$ in the quasi-static, weak-field limit
and using $F'(s) = \tanh(\sqrt{s})$:

$$\boxed{\nabla\cdot\!\left[\tanh\!\left(\frac{a_N}{a_0}\right)\nabla\Phi\right]
= 4\pi G\,\rho_b}$$

with $a_0 = cT_{\rm dS} = cH_0/2\pi$.

**Corollary 1 — Baryonic Tully-Fisher relation.** In the deep-MOND limit
($a_N \ll a_0$), $\tanh(a_N/a_0) \approx a_N/a_0$ and the field equation becomes
$\nabla\cdot[(a_N/a_0)\nabla\Phi] = 4\pi G\rho_b$. For a spherical baryonic mass
$M_b$ at large radius, $a_N^2/a_0 = GM_b/r^2$, giving $a_N = \sqrt{a_0 GM_b}/r$.
The asymptotic circular velocity satisfies:

$$v_c^4 = a_0\,G\,M_b$$

Using $a_0 = cH_0/2\pi$, this gives $v_c = 109$ km/s for $M_b = 10^{10}M_\odot$
and $v_c = 195$ km/s for $M_b = 10^{11}M_\odot$ — consistent with the observed
BTFR zero-point [4,5].

**Corollary 2 — Radial Acceleration Relation.** The MOND equation gives a unique
$a_N$ for each $a_b$ through $a_N\tanh(a_N/a_0) = a_b$, with
$a_N \to a_b$ for $a_b \gg a_0$ and $a_N \to \sqrt{a_b a_0}$ for $a_b \ll a_0$.
This is a parameter-free prediction of the observed RAR [6] (see Fig. 2).

**What is proved.** Given premise (iii) — the AQUAL kinetic action — the MOND
equation follows exactly, with the stated $a_0$. The proof requires:
(i) $\delta\mathcal{C}_K = 3\Phi\mathcal{C}_K^{(0)}$ from the double degeneracy;
(ii) $|\nabla\mathcal{C}_K| = \kappa a_N$;
(iii) $\kappa$ cancels identically;
(iv) $F'(s) = \tanh(\sqrt{s})$ from $C_K = \log\cosh$.

**What remains open.** Premise (iii) — the AQUAL form of the kinetic action —
is supported by the second variation calculation (Section 5.5) but not yet
derived from the DSSYK bulk. The required calculation is to show that the DSSYK
generating functional, as a function of the bulk metric, satisfies:

$$\delta^2\mathcal{C}_{dS}\big[\,g + 2\Phi g\,\big]
\xrightarrow[\text{dict.}]{\text{holo.}}
-\frac{a_0^2}{8\pi G}\int F\!\left(\frac{a_N^2}{a_0^2}\right)d^4x$$

This requires showing the DSSYK boundary time responds to $|\nabla\Phi|$
(gradient) rather than $|\Phi|$ (value) at each bulk point — an explicit
calculation on the DSSYK boundary theory that we defer to future work.

### 5.5 Evidence from the second variation

To support premise (iii), we partially compute $\delta^2\mathcal{C}_{dS}/\delta g^2$.
Two results are relevant.

**The Jacobi spectrum.** The Jacobi operator — governing shape deformations of the
limiting surface — has eigenvalues $\lambda(k,\ell) = 3k^2 - \frac{3}{2}\ell(\ell+1) + \frac{7}{3}$
for mode $(k, \ell)$ in (boundary wavenumber, angular momentum). The ground state
$\lambda_0 = 7/3 \neq 0$: there is no Jacobi near-zero mode. The MOND
enhancement does not come from a shape instability of the LES.

**The non-analytic MOND term.** The MOND signal arises instead from the triple
derivative $g'''(\tau_*) = 208\sqrt{2}/9$ acting through the double degeneracy.
The third-order expansion of the growth rate gives:

$$g = E_*\left[1 + 3\Phi + \frac{13\sqrt{2}}{2}\,|\Phi|^{3/2} + O(\Phi^2)\right]$$

The non-analytic $|\Phi|^{3/2}$ term matches the deep-MOND AQUAL structure
$F(s) \sim s^{3/2}$ when $|\Phi| \sim (a_N/a_0)^2$, which holds at the MOND
transition radius $r_M = \sqrt{GM_b/a_0}$. This is the non-analytic family
structure that produces the MOND behaviour — and it arises precisely from the
double degeneracy identified in Section 3.3. Completing the holographic
dictionary to show this globally (not just at $r_M$) is the remaining step.

## 6. Comparison with SPARC Rotation Curves

### 6.1 The prediction

With $\mu = \tanh$ and $a_0 = cH_0/2\pi$, the CODA prediction for the circular
velocity at each radius is: solve $a_N\tanh(a_N/a_0) = a_b(r)$ for $a_N$, then
$v_c = \sqrt{a_N r}$. The baryonic acceleration is:

$$a_b(r) = \frac{V_{\rm gas}|V_{\rm gas}| + \Upsilon_d V_{\rm disk}^2
+ \Upsilon_b V_{\rm bul}^2}{r}$$

from the SPARC mass models [3], with canonical stellar mass-to-light ratios
$\Upsilon_d = 0.5\,M_\odot/L_\odot$ and $\Upsilon_b = 0.7\,M_\odot/L_\odot$ [24].
The sign-preserving convention $V|V|/r$ handles the (361) negative $V_{\rm gas}$
entries in the SPARC data. No parameters are fitted.

### 6.2 Results across 175 galaxies

We fit all 175 SPARC galaxies (3391 data points) with three models — denoted
CODA (tanh, $a_0 = cH_0/2\pi$), tanh (tanh, $a_0 = 1.2\times10^{-10}$ m/s²),
and simple ($\mu = x/\sqrt{1+x^2}$ [17], $a_0 = 1.2\times10^{-10}$ m/s²):

| Model | Mean $\chi^2_r$ | Median $\chi^2_r$ | Mean RMS (km/s) | $\chi^2_r < 5$ |
|-------|-----------------|-------------------|-----------------|----------------|
| CODA | 40.6 | 11.2 | 18.5 | 58/175 |
| tanh ($a_0 = $ obs) | 36.9 | 10.6 | 18.0 | 56/175 |
| simple ($a_0 = $ obs) | 34.2 | 9.8 | 17.5 | 59/175 |

The $\chi^2_r$ values are elevated compared to standard MOND fits because
mass-to-light ratios are fixed at canonical values rather than fitted per galaxy.
Standard MOND fits with one or two free stellar M/L ratios typically achieve median
$\chi^2_r \sim 1$–5 [18]; our fixed-M/L approach intentionally removes this freedom
to give a cleaner test of the interpolating function shape. The relevant comparison
is between models, not the absolute values.

Stratified by Hubble type:

| Type | $n$ | CODA med. $\chi^2_r$ | tanh obs | simple obs |
|------|-----|----------------------|----------|------------|
| Early (T ≤ 3) | 28 | 49.0 | 45.5 | 34.3 |
| Late (T = 4–7) | 66 | 11.8 | 10.1 | 9.0 |
| Dwarf (T ≥ 8) | 81 | **7.7** | 7.7 | 7.9 |

The dwarf galaxy result (T ≥ 8, 81 galaxies) is the most meaningful comparison:
in the deep-MOND regime where both functions should be most accurate, $\tanh$
narrowly outperforms the simple function (7.69 vs. 7.88). The early-type deficit
for CODA relative to the observed-$a_0$ models reflects the 10% discrepancy in
$a_0$, which has most impact in high-acceleration early-type galaxies near the
transition. See Figs. 3–4 for representative rotation curves and the RAR.

### 6.3 The discriminant at Euclid precision

The two interpolating functions differ most at the transition $a_N = a_0$:

$$\mu_{\tanh}(1) = \tanh(1) \approx 0.762, \qquad
\mu_{\rm simple}(1) = 1/\sqrt{2} \approx 0.707 \quad (+7.7\%)$$

Individual galaxy rotation curves cannot resolve this difference when mass-to-light
ratios are uncertain at the $\sim$20% level. However, Euclid weak-lensing stacking
of galaxies in narrow $a_b/a_0$ bins near the transition will reach $\sim$5%
precision [25,26], making the discrimination accessible. This is the key
observational test.

## 7. Discussion

### 7.1 Relation to existing MOND frameworks

Among the common MOND interpolating functions (summarised in [18]):

| Function | $\mu(x)$ | $\mu(1)$ | Source |
|----------|----------|----------|--------|
| CODA (this work) | $\tanh(x)$ | 0.762 | Derived from $dC_K/dx$ |
| Simple | $x/\sqrt{1+x^2}$ | 0.707 | Phenomenological [17] |
| Standard | $x/(1+x)$ | 0.500 | Original Milgrom [1] |

The tanh function was previously used as a MOND interpolating function on
phenomenological grounds [17,27]; some analyses found it among the better
one-parameter fits to SPARC [28]. This paper provides the first derivation of
this specific form from a theoretical framework, identifying it as the derivative
of the DSSYK Krylov complexity function.

Among relativistic completions, AeST [10] is the closest structural analogue to
CODA. AeST postulates a kinetic Lagrangian $\mathcal{F}(K_{\mu\nu\alpha\beta})$
for a vector field $A^\mu$ that reduces to AQUAL in the quasi-static limit. CODA
produces the same quasi-static reduction from the holographic Krylov density,
with $F$ derived from $C_K = \log\cosh$ rather than postulated, and without
introducing a fundamental vector field. However, AeST's vector field $A^\mu$
gives the correct gravitational lensing angle (equal to the dynamical MOND
prediction) by construction, whereas CODA's current framework — without the
analogue vector sector — does not guarantee lensing-dynamics equivalence. This
is an important open problem for the relativistic extension of the present work.

Verlinde's emergent gravity [9] is the closest conceptual predecessor: it derives
$a_0 \sim cH_0$ from the entropic response of de Sitter to mass concentrations.
CODA uses the Krylov complexity gradient $|\nabla\mathcal{C}_K|$ in place of
Verlinde's displacement of entropy, and produces a specific functional form for
$\mu$ that entropy-based approaches do not determine. Whether the Krylov complexity
density is directly related to the entropic displacement field of [9] is a natural
direction for future work.

### 7.2 The $2\pi$ factor and the holographic dictionary

The three DSSYK routes to $a_0$ give different values: $cH_0$ (Routes 1 and 2)
vs. $cH_0/2\pi$ (Route 3). The factor of $2\pi$ is the thermal normalisation
$T_{\rm dS} = H_0/2\pi$. Observationally, Route 3 is preferred — both because
$cH_0/2\pi$ is 10% from observed (rather than 540% off as $cH_0$ would be), and
because the de Sitter temperature is the natural thermal scale in holographic
complexity calculations.

Determining which route gives the correct $a_0$ — and tracking the $2\pi$ factor
precisely — requires the holographic dictionary calculation of Section 5.4. The
specific question is: when the DSSYK boundary time is varied to probe a bulk point
$x$ with Newtonian potential $\Phi(x)$, does the response scale with $|\Phi(x)|$
(giving $a_0 = cH_0$ from the amplitude of complexity variations) or with
$|\nabla\Phi(x)|$ (giving $a_0 = cH_0/2\pi$ from the gradient structure)?
The second variation calculation (Section 5.5) provides evidence for gradient
response through the non-analytic $|\Phi|^{3/2}$ term, but completing this argument
globally requires the full holographic bulk-to-boundary map.

### 7.3 Galaxy clusters and lensing

Standard MOND without additional dark matter fails for galaxy clusters [29],
where the inferred dynamical mass exceeds the baryonic mass by a factor of a few
even in the MOND framework. The present work does not resolve this problem; it
inherits it from MOND phenomenology. Within a relativistic CODA framework with a
vector sector $A^\mu$, the cluster problem may be partially addressed — AeST [10]
reproduces GR-level lensing by construction, and its cosmological perturbation
theory gives an acceptable CMB power spectrum. Whether the CODA vector sector can
be constructed to similarly pass cluster and lensing constraints is deferred.

### 7.4 CMB and large-scale structure

The Weyl-squared term in the CODA action is Bach-flat in all Einstein geometries,
so it gives corrections to GR of order $(k/M_P)^2 \sim 10^{-114}$ at CMB
scales — completely unobservable. The MOND kinetic sector is suppressed at high
redshift ($a_N \gg a_0$ everywhere before galaxy formation) and activates only in
the low-acceleration regime of present-day galactic disks. A full perturbation
theory of the MOND sector in CODA — including whether it can match the AeST
results on the CMB power spectrum — is left for future work.

## 8. Conclusions

The MOND interpolating function has been unknown theoretically for four decades.
We have shown that it can be derived from holographic Krylov complexity in de
Sitter spacetime. The derivation produces $\mu(x) = \tanh(x)$ as the derivative
of the DSSYK classical complexity function $C_K(x) = \log\cosh(x)$, and the
Milgrom scale $a_0 = cH_0/2\pi$ from the de Sitter temperature — both without
free parameters.

Three results make the derivation concrete:

**1. The Krylov complexity density in dS$_4$.**
The DSSYK limiting extremal surface in four-dimensional de Sitter sits at
$\tau_* = \sqrt{3/2}$ and yields
$\mathcal{C}_K^{(0)} = 2/(3\sqrt{3}\,G_N\ell^2)$. This is finite, covariant,
dimensionally correct, and state-independent.

**2. The double degeneracy and the gradient relation.**
The limiting surface has a doubly-degenerate critical point — both $\partial f/\partial\tau$
and $g'(\tau_*)$ vanish simultaneously. This forces a non-analytic response to
gravity: $(\delta\tau)^2 = -3\Phi/4$, leading to the gradient relation
$|\nabla\mathcal{C}_K| = \kappa a_N$. The coupling $\kappa$ cancels identically
in the AQUAL action, making the MOND equation independent of the holographic
normalisation.

**3. Tanh from log cosh.**
Differentiating $C_K(x) = \log\cosh(x)$ gives $\mu(x) = \tanh(x)$ with
the correct deep-MOND ($\mu \to x$) and Newtonian ($\mu \to 1$) asymptotes.
The baryonic Tully-Fisher relation $v_c^4 = a_0 GM_b$ follows in the deep-MOND
limit.

Testing $\mu = \tanh$ with $a_0 = cH_0/2\pi$ against 175 SPARC galaxies (no free
parameters), we find fits competitive with the standard simple function. In the
most important regime — dwarf galaxies deep in the MOND limit — $\mu = \tanh$
narrowly outperforms simple. The 7.7% difference at the transition ($a_N = a_0$)
is accessible to Euclid weak-lensing stacking at $\sim$5% precision.

The primary open problem is deriving the AQUAL form of the kinetic action from
the DSSYK second variation. The evidence is in hand: the non-analytic
$|\Phi|^{3/2}$ term has the correct power for deep-MOND AQUAL, and the Jacobi
ground eigenvalue $\lambda_0 = 7/3$ shows the MOND signal is a genuine
non-analytic family effect, not a shape instability. Closing this step — mapping
$|\Phi|^{3/2}$ in the DSSYK generating functional to $F(a_N^2/a_0^2)$ via the
holographic dictionary — would complete the first-principles derivation of MOND
from quantum complexity.
